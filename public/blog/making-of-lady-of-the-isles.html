The making of Lady of the Isles

I figured the first blog post should be about how this site got made.

The beginning

On my first year of University, we made a portfolio page on a multimedia class. We couldn't use javascript, and the page was exclusively about some pictures we had taken earlier in the class, but it was the most enjoyable part of the course so far. Coding is like solving a puzzle for me. Setting myself a destination and figuring out how to get there is my favorite part of the creative process. Add to that the instant visual feedback of web and interface development, and it's the perfect niche for my kind of neurodivergence.

At the end of 2025, I stumbled into magoonka's html tutorial and learned about Neocities. I started poking around, and quickly grew fascinated with what I was seeing. They reminded me of all the sites I saw on the computer when I was little, back in the early aughts. I was admittedly not there for the peak of the early web. When I was old enough to read and write, Facebook was already around. But I still faintly remember the tail end of the scene, of playing educational games on incredibly small websites linked on my school's old homepage.

So, right then, I decided I would try my hand at making a website of my own.

First attempt

When I got back from Christmas break, I immediately made myself a Neocities account and set out trying to make a site. At the time, I figured I would just find a pre-made layout online that I liked, and go from there.

This was a mistake.

I downloaded the Haze layout from lilac lynx's resources page, and started customizing it to try to fit it to the type of page I wanted to make. This is not necessarily a bad idea. If you're a complete beginner and want to make a simple site for yourself without too much hassle, using an available free to use layout is fine. I, however, wanted to make a site where I could host a blog with several posts and an index of said posts, and I wanted it all to be generated through the use of the static site generator Eleventy. Which meant having to alter a whole bunch of the bones of a layout that I wasn't very familiar with.

So eventually, after several weeks of trying to drive a proverbial square peg through a round hole, I abandoned the idea of using a pre-made layout.

The pit of despair

There is a point (or several) in every single one of my creative projects that I not so endearingly call "the pit of despair". I know where I'm headed, I know why I'm stuck, but I can't for the life of me figure out how to move on. That is where I found myself after I ditched all the work I had already done. It's pretty normal for me to not touch a project for weeks or even months after I enter the pit. Some of the projects that make it down there never come back out. The pit is not a fun place to be in. There is shame, and so much guilt inside the pit. I would come back from class at the end of the day, turn on my desktop, and see the "projects" tab folder on my browser with all of the inspiration pages and Eleventy documentation still open. And the guilt would quietly gnaw at me. You'd think that it would maybe motivate me to finish what I've started, but that's not how it works. When you're in the pit, everything works to keep you down there.

There are only three ways to leave the pit: I either give up, I fight tooth and nail to climb out of it, or I watch a movie. This is the part where I tell you I watched Project Hail Mary recently. There's something about consuming a really good story that always leaves me with the itch to create. So I opened the project folder, and started from scratch.

Second wind

I started out by creating a list of the things I wanted on the site. I already had a vague idea, but writing it down always makes it easier to define what I need to do in concrete, achievable tasks. I like to use pen and paper for my notes, but if you're more digitally inclined, Obsidian is a phenomenal notes app.

From there, I set about completing all the tasks on my list. I started entirely from scratch, which gave me the freedom to construct the page how I wanted. I decided to go with an admittedly pretty generic design of two side columns and one main column in the center, but it works for my purposes. After I had decided on a layout, I had to figure out how I wanted my page to look. So off I went browsing.

Finding inspiration

Browsing for inspiration isn't a particularly involved process. You browse the web looking for sites that you think look cool and write down what you like about them. Here's a couple of the sites I had on my inspiration list:

  • PetraPixel: A delightfully girly website and a tribute to the old web that does not compromise on accessibility. I'm not a very big fan of pink myself, but this site was one of the biggest inspirations for the layout and content distribution of my own.
  • Ghostk.id: Another big inspiration for my page's layout. I also liked the simple but really well executed grayscale aesthetic.
  • Lilac lynx: I really appreciate the simplicity of this one. Even though I did not end up using their layout, it still served as a big inspiration. I still use the same main font on this version of the site.
  • Slushie Cafe: Another really cute website! Their little pixel art gifs of their OCs are the inspiration for my pixel art icons.

With inspirations gathered, I started working on the site's visual style. It looks a little simpler than I'd like, but I also understand that I had to set a "good enough" limit for myself, or I would never launch this site. In the future, I intend to keep updating this site visually.

Code wrangling

After the site's baseline structure and visual style was laid down, I wanted to set up a static site generator. Technically I don't need one, but that's like saying I technically don't need transportation for crossing the ocean. Setting up Eleventy is a little time-consuming and frustrating, but once the initial setup is done, it saves you a tremendous amount of time. Eleventy handles building the pages for you with the pre-made base components you have made, which allows you to only focus on the parts that are unique between pages. If you have more patience than me, you can even set it up so you can write your pages in a simpler format like markdown, and it will handle converting it to html automatically for you. Shout out once again to PetraPixel! Her Eleventy tutorial is an awesome intro to the tool and it made the initial setup much easier.

With the initial set up done, the real bulk of the work begins: building the site. This involved breaking down the sample html page I had made into components Eleventy could use to build a page, and after that was done, starting to build the pages I wanted to include in my site. This was without a doubt the part of the process that took the longest. You'd be surprised at how difficult it is to write about yourself. Every sentence leaves you second-guessing whether you're oversharing or if it makes you sound like the most insufferable person on the planet. I think I've managed to strike a nice balance though.

Closing thoughts

So... after all that work, the site is ready for launch. When I started writing this blogpost, I had perhaps somewhat ambitiously set up the launch date for May 6. At time of writing, the calendar reads May 7. Oops. I don't think I'll be off by too long though, I'm getting pretty close. Just a few final touches and revisions, and we'll be done.

Why did I go through all this work? Well, for a couple reasons. I wanted a place where I can share with the world the things I do and say. I wanted it to be truly mine, where the only person I have to answer to is myself, and not some algorithm tasked with deciding whether or not I'm worthy of clicks. Was this process fun? At times. I'd be lying if I said that the creative process is painless though. I mean, I wrote an entire section about it after all.

I hope that this site serves as an inspiration to someone else. I hope other people stumble into it, and see that the Internet is still more than the handful of media giants forcing us to stew in a pot of our own misery. I hope that, going forward, I can keep making things. And as completely naive as I know this sounds, I hope I can make the world better. Even if it is just an infinitely miniscule amount. That is more than enough for me.
